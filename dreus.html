When Conversations Become Necessary

Published on January 5, 2026 | By Dreus

Rumi was a teacher, comfortable in his role, secure in his routine. Then Shams-e Tabrizi walked into his classroom, and everything Rumi thought he knew about connection shattered. This wasn't someone new to his life—Shams was simply another seeker of knowledge. But when their minds met, when their conversations began, Rumi discovered that the person who would change his entire world had been right there in his teaching circle.

"Your task is not to seek for love," Rumi writes, because sometimes you don't have to seek it. Sometimes it's sitting across from you in study sessions, asking questions that make you think differently, challenging you in ways that wake something up inside you. Sometimes the person you've been looking for has been in the same room all along.

What makes Rumi's transformation so powerful is how unexpected it was. Shams wasn't someone exotic or distant—he was just another student of life. But their connection turned ordinary discussions into something transcendent. Suddenly every shared conversation felt necessary. Every moment apart felt like loss. The familiar classroom became charged with new meaning.

This is the love that catches you off guard precisely because there's no distance to romanticize it. They're not a mysterious stranger—you've seen them struggle, make mistakes, be human. You've shared boring moments, tedious lectures, ordinary days. And somehow that makes it more profound when you realize you've fallen for them. Because you've fallen for the real person, not a fantasy.

Rumi's 40,000 verses for Shams remind us that the most transformative connections often begin in the most ordinary spaces. The classmate you study with. The person you partner with for projects. The one whose perspective in discussions you start to wait for. Sometimes love doesn't arrive dramatically—it just slowly becomes undeniable, built from a thousand small moments of recognition. —Dreus
